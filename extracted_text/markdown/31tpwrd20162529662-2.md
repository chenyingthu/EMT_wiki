# Partial refactorization based machine modeling techniques for electromagnetic transients

U. Karaagac, Member, J. Mahseredjian, Fellow, I. Kocar, Member, G. Soykan, Member, O. Saad, Member

Abstract— Electromagnetic transient type (EMT-type) programs solve nodal analysis (NA) or modified-augmentednodal-analysis (MANA) formulation based power system network equations (PSNE). The PSNE coefficient matrix can be either constant or time-varying depending on the utilized synchronous machine (SM) model. Therefore, a computational efficiency comparison for the SM models requires accounting for the refactorization scheme and the utilized sparse matrix solver (SMS). In addition, MANA formulation does not require or force admittance model usage and enables flexibility while representing the SMs in PSNE. This paper compares computational performances of various SM models for a practical case and accounts for the refactorization scheme, the utilized SMS and the SM representation in MANA formulation.

Index Terms—Electromagnetic transients, modifiedaugmented-nodal-analysis (MANA), synchronous machine, partial refactorization, sparse matrix solver

## I. INTRODUCTION

he nodal analysis (NA) formulation based power system network model has been widely used in Electromagnetic transient type (EMT-type) programs. Despite its formulation efficiency, NA has several important limitations resulting from the existence of admittance model assumption. The power system network equations (PSNE) in NA formulation becomes variable rank to model the ideal switch operations. In addition, direct representation of the branch relations and the devices with voltage and current relations is not possible. These limitations can be eliminated using the modified-augmentednodal-analysis (MANA) formulation [1]-[3] and that is why this paper is based on MANA formulation.

In both NA and MANA formulations, the PSNE coefficient matrix is sparse and the solution of PSNE is based on sparse matrix methods and LU factorization [4].

In both NA and MANA formulations, the synchronous machines (SMs) are modeled separately from network equations and require a special interface. A common approach is to interface the SM models with PSNE by using Norton equivalent (NE) circuits. The NE circuits of the SMs are calculated and inserted into PSNE by modifying the network admittance matrix and the vector of history current sources. Following the PSNE solution, electrical and mechanical variables of the SMs are calculated using the SM terminal voltages.

The classical dq0 model [5] of SM is widely used in the computation of EMTs, and interfaced with PSNE using a Thevenin prediction-based circuit equivalent [6]-[10]. This model uses a constant admittance matrix and offers the best computational speed.

In the phase-domain (PD) [11]-[13] and voltage-behindreactance (VBR) [14][15] model structures, the stator circuit is represented in phase coordinates and directly included within PSNE. These approaches improve numerical accuracy and stability [7]-[10], [15], [16]. However, due to time varying inductances of these models, it is necessary to update and refactor the PSNE at each solution time point and so causing significant computational speed deterioration.

The recent SM models, proposed in [8] (PD-dq0 model) and [10] (VBR-dq0 model) are obtained by applying Park’s transformation to the discretized equations of PD and VBR models, respectively. These models maintain the precisions of accurate models and eliminate their computational inefficiencies through a constant admittance matrix.

In [10], the performances of dq0, PD-dq0, VBR-dq0, PD and VBR models have been tested on a 30-machine practical case for both precision and efficiency of simulation. This study shows that, the computation time increases more than twice in EMTP (EMTP-RV version) [2][3] when PD or VBR model is utilized instead of $\mathrm { d } { \bf q } 0$ , PD-dq0 or VBR-dq0 for the same numerical integration time step. A similar increase in the computation time of EMTP has been also reported in [7] for a five machine practical case.

The PD model proposed in [17] also achieves a constant admittance matrix usage in the SM-network interface. The accuracy assessment in [17] is based on single-phase-toground fault simulation results for a SM directly connected to an infinite bus. The authors left the computational efficiency assessment of this model as future study. As the computational performance of this model has not been validated for a multimachine practical system, and due to its inclusion of artificial damper windings, it is not considered in this paper.

As in the dq0 model, constant admittance matrix usage in PD-dq0 and VBR-dq0 models is achieved using stator current predictions. Prediction errors will, however, increase with larger time steps and can cause numerical errors or instability in some cases. The dq0 model uses damping resistances [6] - [10] to correct numerical problems caused by prediction. A practical case is presented in [10] where the prediction errors in the dq0 model cause numerical accuracy and stability problems for large simulation time steps. The prediction errors do not cause such a numerical problem in PD-dq0 and VBRdq0 models for the same test case [10]. However, this is still an issue since the damping resistances may still be needed in some cases to correct numerical problems.

An alternative approach to damping resistance usage is the implementation of an iterative solution loop with the PSNE [10]. In this approach the machine Thevenin equivalent (TE) of the SM is updated for voltage convergence through an iterative process [2], [3] that includes all other nonlinear devices and machines connected through the main network. The iterative solution used in [10] caused an increase of only 20% in the 30-machine test case, when using a very low relative convergence tolerance.

EMTP uses a complete refactorization scheme and a SMS that employs multiple minimum degree ordering (MMD) to obtain sparser LU factors. The hypothesis is that the computational performances of PD and VBR models might be improved significantly through a partial refactorization scheme. Depending on the computational gain with partial refactorization, PD and VBR models might be preferred to their counterparts (PD-dq0 and VBR-dq0) as these models do not contain predicted stator currents and eliminate the possible requirement of either damping resistance usage or network iterative solution option.

The above hypothesis (see also [17]) has never been implemented and verified before this paper.

This paper compares the numerical performances of the PDdq0 (with and without network iterative solution), PD and VBR models by considering the employed SMS and refactorization scheme. In addition, this paper proposes two new MANA formulations for PD and VBR models. In the first formulation, machine TEs and in the second formulation all machine electrical equations, are inserted into the PSNE. The dq0 and VBR-dq0 models are not considered in this paper as both models use constant admittance matrix and provide similar computational performance with the PD-dq0 model [10].

The SM models, the proposed MANA formulations and the partial refactorization scheme for PD and VBR models are implemented in MatEMTP (a transient analysis program in MATLAB m-files) [1]. In addition, two different SMSs are used for the solution of PSNE: LU factorization with approximate minimum degree ordering (AMD) [18][19] and KLU Matrix-solver package [20].

The first part of this paper briefly recalls the discrete-time model equations for PD and VBR models. The second part presents the proposed MANA formulations. The KLU package is presented briefly in the third part. The forth part demonstrates the partial factorization implementation and the simulation studies are presented in the last part.

## II.SYNCHRONOUS MACHINE EQUATIONS

The damper winding effects are represented with three lumped-parameter damper windings: one on d-axis, and two on q-axis. The d-axis is assumed to be leading the q-axis by $9 0 ^ { 0 }$ and the generator convention is used for machine equations [21]. Throughout this paper, bold uppercase denotes matrices, bold lowercase denotes vectors and the operator $p$ is $d / d t$ Reader should refer to [9] for the mechanical equations.

The machine models are implemented using the trapezoidal rule of integration. The numerical oscillations of this rule, due to detectable discontinuities, are eliminated using the Backward Euler method. An operating segment change on the saturation curve is also treated as a discontinuity.

Due to space limitations, the PD-dq0 model equations and the network iterative solution approach (PD-dq0-IT model) are not presented in this paper. The reader should refer to [8]-[10].

## III. MANA FORMULATION

The standard MANA formulation in [1]-[3] is augmented to include either machine TE circuits or machine electrical equations in addition to the generalized nodal relations for the machine-network connection point:

$$
\left[ \begin{array} { c c c c c } { \mathbf { Y } _ { \mathbf { n } } } & { \mathbf { V } _ { \mathbf { a d j } } ^ { T } } & { \mathbf { D } _ { \mathbf { b d e p c } } } & { \mathbf { S } _ { \mathbf { a d j } } ^ { T } } & { \mathbf { S } \mathbf { M } _ { \mathbf { a d j } } ^ { T } } \\ { \mathbf { V } _ { \mathbf { a d j } } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } \\ { \mathbf { D } _ { \mathbf { b d e p r } } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } \\ { \mathbf { S } _ { \mathbf { a d j } } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { S } _ { \mathbf { 0 } } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } \\ { \mathbf { S } \mathbf { M } _ { \mathbf { a d j } } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { 0 } } & { \mathbf { A } _ { \mathbf { S M } } } \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array} { c } { \mathbf { v } _ { \mathbf { n } } } \\ { \mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { V } \mathbf { S } } } \\ { \mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { V } \mathbf { d } } } \\ { \mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { S } } } \\ { \mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { S } } } \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array} { c } { \mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { n } } } \\ { \mathbf { V } _ { \mathbf { S } } } \\ { \mathbf { 0 } } \\ { \mathbf { 0 } } \\ { \mathbf { b } _ { \mathbf { S M } } } \end{array} \right]\tag{22}
$$

where ${ \bf Y _ { n } }$ is the admittance matrix, ${ \bf v _ { n } }$ is the vector of node voltages, $\mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { n } }$ is the vector of current sources combined with history current sources for the trapezoidal integration method, $\mathbf { V _ { a d j } }$ is the voltage source adjacency matrix, $\mathbf { D _ { b d e p r } }$ and $\mathbf { D _ { b d e p c } }$ are row and column contribution matrices used for holding the branch dependent relations, $\mathbf { S _ { a d j } }$ is the adjacency matrix of switch type devices, $\bf { S _ { 0 } }$ is a diagonal and unitary matrix for open switch type devices, $\bf i _ { V s }$ is the vector of unknown voltage source currents, $\mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { V } \mathbf { d } }$ is the vector of unknown currents in dependent voltage source branches and $\mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { S } }$ is the vector of unknown switch currents. The above presentation is only for analysis purposes since in reality the equations can be entered in any order. It is also noticed that the matrix $\bf { S _ { 0 } }$ is not necessarily unitary if switch resistances are used. Extra elements can be added in the switch rows for modeling various devices.

In the standard MANA approach, the machines are incorporated by modifying $\mathbf { Y _ { n } }$ and $\mathbf { i } _ { \mathbf { n } }$ with the machine NE obtained from the TE given in (6) and (19) for PD and VBR models, respectively. The new approach proposed in this paper is demonstrated below.

## A. MANA Formulation with Machine Thevenin Equivalents (MANA-T)

In MANA-T, (22) includes the SM TE circuits, i.e. $\mathbf { A _ { S M } }$ holds the equivalent Thevenin resistance matrices (see (7) and (20) for PD and VBR models), $\mathbf { x _ { S M } }$ is the vector of stator currents and $\mathbf { b } _ { \mathbf { S M } }$ is the vector of Thevenin voltages (see (8) and (21) for PD and VBR models). $\mathbf { S M _ { a d j } }$ is the machine adjacency matrix.

MANA-T eliminates the computation of NE from TE for each machine at each solution time point. Moreover, the solution of (22) now provides the SM stator currents in addition to SM terminal voltages. Hence the calculation of stator currents following the PSNE solution is also eliminated. As the PD-dq0 model uses a constant Norton admittance matrix, MANA-T should not provide significant improvement in simulation speed for this model. MANA-T increases the size of the PSNE coefficient matrix and may impact on the PSNE solution time.

## B. MANA Formulation with Machine Complete Electrical Equations (MANA-C)

In MANA-C, (22) includes all machine electrical equations in addition to the generalized nodal relations for the machinenetwork connection points. So the computation of machine interfacing circuitry is completely eliminated. Moreover, the solution of (22) now provides all machine electrical variables in addition to machine terminal voltages. It should be noted that, MANA-C introduces time dependent terms in (22) when used for the PD-dq0 model. As the advantage of the constant PSNE coefficient matrix usage disappears, MANA-C is not suitable for PD-dq0 model.

MANA-C increases both the size and the number of time varying terms in the PSNE coefficient matrix. Hence, a significant increase is expected in the required time for updating and refactoring the PSNE coefficient matrix.

## IV. KLU MATRIX-SOLVE PACKAGE

The solution of linear equation system  Ax = b can be found by solving two triangular system obtained with LU factorization, i.e.

$$
\mathbf { A } \mathbf { x } = ( \mathbf { L } \mathbf { U } ) \mathbf { x } = \mathbf { L } ( \mathbf { U } \mathbf { x } ) = \mathbf { L } \mathbf { y } = \mathbf { b }\tag{25}
$$

where  U and  L are the upper and lower triangular matrices, respectively.

By employing special ordering techniques to generate the row and column permutations, it is possible to reduce the fillin during factorization.

$$
\mathbf { P _ { R } } \ \mathbf { A } \ \mathbf { P _ { C } } = \mathbf { L } \ \mathbf { U }\tag{26}
$$

Here, $\mathbf { P _ { C } }$ and $\mathbf { P _ { R } }$ are the column and row permutation matrices, respectively. It should be noted that the permutation $\mathbf { P _ { R } }$ has a dual role of reducing fill in and maintaining numerical accuracy via partial pivoting and row interchanges. Minimum degree ordering, the most popular ordering technique, such as AMD [18][19] or COLAMD [22][23] can be applied to obtain sparser  L and  U matrices.

The KLU [20] solver permutes the matrix  A  to obtain the block triangular form (BTF) before ordering. Ordering and factorization of the BTF provides the following advantages:

 The part of the matrix below the block diagonal does not require factorization;

 The diagonal blocks are independent, hence only these blocks require factorization;

 The off-diagonal nonzeros do not contribute to any fill-in.

KLU employs AMD or COLAMD (user option) for ordering before factorization of each diagonal block. In [20], AMD is found to provide better results on circuit matrices. KLU performs factorization based on Gilbert-Peierls' left-looking algorithm [22]. The reader should refer to [20] for details. The

KLU Matrix solve package for MATLAB is available in [25] with the required application notes. The KLU package factorizes the matrix  A as

$$
\mathbf { R } ^ { - 1 } \left( \mathbf { P _ { R } } \ \mathbf { A } \ \mathbf { P _ { C } } \right) = \mathbf { L } \ \mathbf { U } + \mathbf { F }\tag{27}
$$

where F contains the entries above the diagonal blocks and R is a scaling matrix.

## V.PARTIAL RE-FACTORIZATION IMPLEMENTATION

As PD and VBR models introduce time-varying elements in the PSNE coefficient matrix, it is required to update and refactor at each time-step. The idea in partial refactorization is refactoring only the time varying part of PSNE coefficient matrix to improve efficiency.

Partial refactorization in EMT-type solution was first discussed in [26] and [27] for nonlinear branch functions and switches. It is applied here, for the first time, to synchronous machine modeling and in the context of more generic MANA formulation of network equations.

In the proposed formulations given in (22), only the submatrix $\mathbf { A _ { S M } }$ contains the time varying terms and it is located at the right bottom corner of PSNE coefficient matrix. Thus, (22) can be rewritten as

$$
{ \begin{array} { r l } { { \left[ \mathbf { A _ { 1 1 } } \quad \mathbf { A _ { 1 2 } } \right] } { \left[ \mathbf { x _ { 1 } } \right] } = { \left[ \mathbf { b _ { 1 } } \right] } } \\ { \mathbf { A _ { 2 1 } } \quad \mathbf { A _ { 2 2 } } } \end{array} }\tag{28}
$$

where $\mathbf { A _ { 2 2 } } = \mathbf { A _ { S M } } , \ \mathbf { x _ { 2 } } = \mathbf { x _ { S M } }$ and $\mathbf { b } _ { 2 } = \mathbf { b } _ { \mathbf { S M } }$ . It should be noted that ${ \bf A } _ { 2 1 } = { \bf A } _ { 1 2 } ^ { T }$ for this formulation.

When $\mathbf { x } _ { 2 }$ in (28) is known, $\mathbf { x _ { 1 } }$ can be found by solving

$$
\mathbf { A _ { 1 1 } x _ { 1 } } = \mathbf { b _ { 1 } } - \mathbf { A _ { 1 2 } } \mathbf { \Delta x } _ { 2 }\tag{29}
$$

By factoring $\mathbf { A _ { 1 1 } }$ and after some algebraic manipulations, (28) becomes

$$
\begin{array} { r l } { \left[ \begin{array} { c c } { \mathbf { U } } & { \mathbf { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { R } } \mathbf { A } _ { 1 2 } } \\ { \mathbf { A } _ { 2 1 } \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { C } } } & { \mathbf { A } _ { 2 2 } } \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array} { l } { \mathbf { y } _ { 1 } } \\ { \mathbf { x } _ { 2 } } \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array} { l } { \mathbf { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { R } } \mathbf { b } _ { 1 } } \\ { \mathbf { b } _ { 2 } } \end{array} \right] } & { { } } \end{array}\tag{30}
$$

where

$$
\mathbf { y _ { 1 } } = \mathbf { P _ { C } ^ { \textit { T } } } \mathbf { x _ { 1 } }\tag{31}
$$

Let  K be a matrix satisfying the equation

$$
\mathbf { K } \mathbf { U } = - \mathbf { A } _ { 2 1 } \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { C } }\tag{32}
$$

The matrix  K can be found by eliminating the terms in $\mathbf { A } _ { 2 1 } \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { C } }$ using the upper triangular matrix  U . By using equations (30) and (32), and after some algebraic manipulations, the equation system regarding $\mathbf { x } _ { 2 }$ can be found as

$$
\mathbf { A } _ { 2 2 } ^ { \prime } \mathbf { x } _ { 2 } = \mathbf { b } _ { 2 } ^ { \prime }\tag{33}
$$

where

$$
\mathbf { A } _ { 2 2 } ^ { \prime } = \mathbf { A } _ { 2 2 } + \mathbf { K } \mathbf { L } ^ { \mathbf { - 1 } } \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { R } } \mathbf { \Delta } \mathbf { A _ { 1 2 } }\tag{34}
$$

$$
\mathbf { b } _ { 2 } ^ { \prime } = \mathbf { b } _ { 2 } + \mathbf { K } \mathbf { L } ^ { \mathbf { - 1 } } \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { R } } \mathbf { b _ { 1 } }\tag{35}
$$

The matrix ${ \bf A } _ { 2 2 } ^ { \prime }$ must be updated and refactorized at each time-step to solve (33) for $\mathbf { x } _ { 2 }$ $\mathbf { x _ { 1 } }$ can be found using (29) after solving (33). As ${ \bf A } _ { 2 2 } ^ { \prime }$ is re-factorized instead of  A at

each time step, a significant improvement is expected in simulation speed. When the KLU package is used, (32), (34) and (35) become

$$
\textbf { K } \left( \mathbf { U } + \mathbf { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathbf { F } \right) = - \mathbf { A } _ { 2 1 } \ \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { C } }\tag{36}
$$

$$
{ \bf A } _ { 2 2 } ^ { \prime } = { \bf A } _ { 2 2 } + { \bf K } { \bf L } ^ { 1 } { \bf R } ^ { - 1 } { \bf P } _ { \mathrm { R } } { \bf \Sigma } { \bf A } _ { 1 2 }\tag{37}
$$

$$
\mathbf { b } _ { 2 } ^ { \prime } = \mathbf { b } _ { 2 } + \mathbf { K } \mathbf { \Lambda } \mathbf { R } ^ { - 1 } \mathbf { L } ^ { - 1 } \mathbf { P } _ { \mathbf { R } } \mathbf { \Lambda } \mathbf { b _ { 1 } }\tag{38}
$$

Partial refactorization is implemented to the standard MANA formulation in a similar manner. For this purpose, the row and columns of  A are permuted in such a way that the time varying terms are located at the right bottom corner of the matrix. Thus, PSNE having the form given in (28) is obtained. The vectors $\mathbf { x } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathbf { b } _ { 2 }$ in (28) now contain the machine terminal voltages and the known current quantities modified with the machine NE currents, respectively. The sub-matrix $\mathbf { A } _ { 2 2 }$ in (28) now contains the terms modified with the machine NE admittances.

## VII. CONCLUSION

This paper delivered a comprehensive comparison of computational performances for PD-dq0, PD and VBR models on a 30 machine practical system and accounted for the refactorization scheme, the sparse matrix solver and the SM representation in MANA formulation. The new MANA formulations for PD and VBR models are obtained by augmenting the standard MANA formulation to include either SM Thevenin circuits or complete SM electrical equations.

The simulation results show that the total simulation time of EMTP is expected to reduce by 28.5% for the VBR model and 27.7% for the PD model with the partial refactorization scheme. However, even with partial refactorization, the VBR and PD models remain less efficient than the PD-dq0 model. PD-dq0 model provides a computational gain more than 1.37 over VBR model with partial refactorization. When the PDdq0 is used with iterative network solution option (PD-dq0-IT model), this computational gain reduces to 1.21. The computational gain over the PD model (with partial refactorization) is larger as the VBR model is more efficient than the PD model.

The simulation results also show that, the effect of the sparse matrix solver on simulation efficiency strongly depends on the machine model. The KLU sparse matrix solver usage in EMTP instead of LU factorization with MMD is expected to reduce the total simulation time by 25.9% for PD-dq0, 32.4% for PD-dq0-IT, and 16.5% for PD and 17.2% for VBR models with partial refactorization scheme. As a result the computational gains provided by PD-dq0 and PD-dq0-IT models become more than 1.53 and 1.48 over VBR model with partial refactorization, respectively. The decrease in PDdq0 model performance with iterative network solution option becomes less significant with KLU usage. This makes network iterative approach more attractive compared to damping resistance usage for correcting potential numerical problems caused by stator current predictions.

This paper also demonstrated the flexibility of the MANA approach.
